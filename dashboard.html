What Are Prop Firms?
A beginner-friendly guide to proprietary trading firms for futures

A prop firm (short for proprietary trading firm) is a company that gives you a funded trading account using their money — not yours. In the futures world, this means you can trade contracts like NQ, ES, MNQ, and MES without risking your own capital. If you're profitable, you keep 80–90% of the profits. The firm takes a small cut for providing the capital.

Unlike forex or stock prop firms, futures prop firms focus specifically on CME-listed contracts (the same ones Stratford trades). They're popular because they let traders access large accounts — $50K, $100K, even $150K+ — without putting up that money themselves.

🏦 How It Works
1
Pass an Evaluation
Trade a simulated account and hit a profit target — without exceeding the maximum drawdown. This proves you can trade consistently.
2
Get Funded
Once you pass, the firm gives you a real funded account with their capital. You trade live markets with real money — theirs, not yours.
3
Keep Your Profits
You keep 80–90% of every dollar you make. The firm takes a small percentage as their share for providing the capital.
📋 Key Rules to Know
Max Drawdown
The maximum amount your account can lose in total before the firm closes it. For example, a $50K account typically has a $2,000 max drawdown — meaning if your account drops to $48,000, you're done.
Daily Loss Limit
Many firms also cap how much you can lose in a single day. This is usually smaller than the max drawdown. If you hit it, trading is paused until the next day — but your account stays open.

Trading Terminology
Key terms every beginner should know

Futures trading comes with its own vocabulary. You don't need to memorize all of this — Stratford handles the trading for you — but understanding these terms will help you read your dashboard, evaluate strategies, and understand what's happening with your account.

↕️ Trade Direction
Long (Buy)
You profit when the price goes up. The strategy buys a contract expecting the market to rise, then sells it at a higher price.
Short (Sell)
You profit when the price goes down. The strategy sells a contract first, then buys it back at a lower price. In futures, shorting is just as easy as going long.
📝 Key Order & Trade Terms
Entry
When a trade is opened — the strategy buys or sells a contract to start a position.
Exit
When a trade is closed — the position is sold (or bought back) to lock in profit or cut a loss.
Stop Loss
A safety order that automatically closes your trade if the price moves against you by a set amount. Limits how much you can lose on a single trade.
Take Profit
An order that automatically closes your trade once it hits a target profit. Locks in gains without needing to watch the screen.
Position Size
How many contracts you're trading at once. More contracts = more profit potential but also more risk. Stratford picks the right size for your account.
Margin
The amount of money your broker requires to hold a futures position open. It's not the full contract value — just a deposit, like a security deposit on a rental.
📊 Performance Stats
These are the numbers you'll see on the Backtest Results and Strategies pages. Here's what they actually mean:
Win Rate
The percentage of trades that made money. A 72% win rate means 72 out of every 100 trades were profitable.
Profit Factor
Total profits divided by total losses. A profit factor of 3.15 means the strategy made $3.15 for every $1 it lost. Anything above 1.0 is profitable.
Net Profit
The total amount of money made after subtracting all losses. This is the bottom line — how much the strategy earned over the entire backtest period.
Max Drawdown
The largest peak-to-trough decline in account value. If your account hit $50,000 then dipped to $47,000, your max drawdown was $3,000. Lower is better.
Average Trade
The average profit or loss per trade. An avg trade of +$239 means the strategy made $239 on average every time it opened and closed a position.
Sharpe Ratio
Measures return relative to risk. A Sharpe ratio above 1.0 is good, above 2.0 is great. It tells you how much return you're getting per unit of risk taken.
🛡️ Risk & Account Terms
Drawdown
How much your account has dropped from its highest point. Prop firms set a max drawdown limit — exceed it and your account is closed.
Daily Loss Limit
The max you can lose in one day. Hit it and trading stops for the day. This protects you from one bad session wiping out your account.
Evaluation / Challenge
The test phase at a prop firm where you prove you can trade profitably within the rules. Pass it and you get a funded account.
Funded Account
A real trading account provided by a prop firm using their money. You trade it, keep 80–90% of profits, and follow their rules.
Paper Trading / Demo
Simulated trading with fake money. Lets you test a strategy without risking real capital. Stratford offers a free paper demo to try before going live.
Backtest
Running a strategy against historical market data to see how it would have performed. Stratford's backtests span 4+ years of real market conditions.

What Are Indicators?
How trading indicators work and how Stratford uses them

Indicators are mathematical tools that analyze price, volume, and time data to help predict where the market might go next. Think of them as filters — they take raw price movement and turn it into signals a trader (or bot) can act on. You don't need to learn any of this to use Stratford, but it helps to understand what's working behind the scenes.

📐 How Indicators Work
Every indicator takes past price data and runs a formula on it to produce a value — a line on a chart, a number, or a signal (buy/sell). Traders use them to answer questions like: Is the market trending up? Is it overbought? Is momentum slowing down?
Leading Indicators
Try to predict future price movement. They give signals before a move happens, but can produce false signals. Examples: RSI, Stochastic.
Lagging Indicators
Confirm a trend after it starts. They're slower but more reliable — fewer false signals. Examples: Moving Averages, MACD.
📊 Common Indicators Explained
〰️
Moving Average (MA)
TREND
Smooths out price data by averaging the last X candles (e.g., 20, 50, 200). Shows the overall direction of the market. When price is above the MA, the trend is generally up. When below, it's down.
How it's used: Identify trend direction. A "golden cross" (short MA crosses above long MA) signals bullish momentum. A "death cross" is the opposite.
📈
RSI (Relative Strength Index)
MOMENTUM
Measures how fast and how much the price has moved recently, on a scale of 0–100. Above 70 = overbought (price may drop). Below 30 = oversold (price may rise).
How it's used: Spot potential reversals. When RSI hits extreme levels, the market often pulls back. Also used to confirm trend strength.
📉
MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence)
TREND + MOMENTUM
Compares two moving averages and plots the difference. When the MACD line crosses above the signal line, it's bullish. When it crosses below, it's bearish. The histogram shows the strength of the move.
How it's used: Identify trend changes and momentum shifts. One of the most popular indicators for both manual and automated strategies.
🔲
Bollinger Bands
VOLATILITY
Three lines: a moving average in the middle, and upper/lower bands that expand and contract based on market volatility. When bands are tight, a big move is coming. When price touches the outer bands, it may reverse.
How it's used: Gauge volatility and spot potential breakouts. A "squeeze" (tight bands) often precedes a sharp move in either direction.
📶
Volume
CONFIRMATION
The number of contracts traded in a given time period. High volume confirms that a price move is real and supported by many traders. Low volume moves are weaker and more likely to reverse.
How it's used: Confirm breakouts and trend strength. A breakout on high volume is much more reliable than one on low volume.
📏
ATR (Average True Range)
VOLATILITY
Measures the average range of price movement over a set period. Doesn't tell you direction — just how much the market is moving. Higher ATR = more volatile market.
How it's used: Set dynamic stop losses and take profits. Instead of a fixed dollar amount, you base your risk on how volatile the market actually is.
🧩 How Indicators Work Together
No single indicator is perfect on its own. Professional strategies — including Stratford's — combine multiple indicators to filter out noise and confirm signals. Here's how they work as a team:
🧭
Trend Filter
A Moving Average determines the overall market direction. Only take long trades in uptrends and short trades in downtrends.
🎯
Entry Signal
RSI or MACD spots the exact moment to enter — when momentum aligns with the trend direction and conditions are favorable.
🛡️
Risk Management
ATR or Bollinger Bands set dynamic stop losses and targets based on current volatility, so risk is always proportional.
Think of it like a checklist: The strategy won't enter a trade unless multiple indicators agree. This "confluence" approach dramatically reduces false signals and improves the win rate.
